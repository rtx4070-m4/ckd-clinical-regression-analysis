Abstract

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a progressive condition affecting over 800 million individuals globally, characterized by a gradual decline in renal function. Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) remains the gold standard for assessing kidney function and staging CKD severity. This study develops a multivariate regression framework for estimating GFR using six clinical biomarkers: age, serum creatinine, blood urea nitrogen (BUN) levels, hemoglobin levels, systolic blood pressure, and protein in urine. A dataset comprising 1,659 patient observations was preprocessed through median and mode imputation, StandardScaler normalization, and robust outlier detection using Median Absolute Deviation (MAD). Feature selection was conducted using LASSO regularization, principal component analysis (PCA), and forward stepwise regression. Four regression models were developed and compared: ordinary least squares (OLS) linear regression, LASSO regression, Ridge regression, and principal component regression (PCR). Ridge regression emerged as the best-performing model (R² = 0.76, SSE = 4,800, AIC = 4,030), with serum creatinine identified as the strongest predictor of GFR (r = −0.92). These findings support the clinical utility of regularized regression approaches for renal function estimation.

1. Introduction

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) represents one of the most pressing global public health challenges. Defined by a sustained reduction in kidney function over three months or longer, CKD affects an estimated 10% of the global adult population and is a leading contributor to cardiovascular morbidity and all-cause mortality. The disease progresses through five stages, classified primarily by the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).

The objective of this study is to develop and evaluate a multivariate regression framework for estimating GFR in CKD patients using six routinely collected clinical biomarkers. By comparing OLS, LASSO, Ridge, and PCR models, we seek to identify the optimal modeling strategy and the most informative predictors for non-invasive GFR estimation.

2. Methodology

The dataset comprises 1,659 observations with seven variables: age, serum creatinine, BUN levels, hemoglobin levels, systolic blood pressure, protein in urine, and GFR (target). Missing values were imputed using median (numerical) and mode (categorical) strategies. All predictors were standardized using StandardScaler. Outlier detection via MAD identified zero outliers. Feature selection was performed via LASSO (optimal α = 1.38), PCA, and forward stepwise selection (AIC-based). Four regression models were trained on an 80/20 train-test split and evaluated using SSE, R², and AIC.

3. Results

Ridge regression achieved the highest R² (0.76), lowest SSE (4,800), and lowest AIC (4,030). Serum creatinine exhibited the strongest bivariate correlation with GFR (r = −0.92). BUN levels (r = −0.41), protein in urine (r = −0.34), hemoglobin (r = 0.27), age (r = −0.18), and systolic BP (r = −0.09) provided complementary predictive information. Residual diagnostics confirmed homoscedasticity, approximate normality, and VIF values below 2.5.

4. Discussion

The dominance of serum creatinine as the strongest predictor is consistent with established nephrology research. The superiority of Ridge regression over LASSO and OLS is attributable to its L2 penalty, which effectively manages multicollinearity without discarding predictors. Hemoglobin's positive association with GFR reflects the kidney's erythropoietin production role. Proteinuria captures glomerular barrier dysfunction, a mechanistically distinct dimension of CKD. Future work should explore cystatin C integration and external multi-center validation.

5. Conclusion

Ridge regression emerged as the optimal model for GFR estimation (R² = 0.76), outperforming LASSO, OLS, and PCR. Serum creatinine was identified as the most powerful predictor (r = −0.92), followed by BUN, proteinuria, hemoglobin, age, and systolic BP. The framework's reliance on routinely available measurements enhances its deployability in resource-constrained settings, contributing to improved CKD screening and monitoring.
